Chapter 9 
Mechanical Design 
Structural design, load analysis, material selection, fabrication, and validation of the robotic arm. 
9.1 Introduction 
In this chapter we walk through the mechanical design of our robotic arm and how we validated it. 
The arm is what physically does the work in our system: it carries the end-effector across the 
workspace, holds it still while the camera identifies objects, and moves the gripped ball over to the 
right container. Every other part of the project — perception, control, integration — only matters if 
the arm itself doesn't break under load. That's why we treated the mechanical side as the 
foundation everything else sits on. 
We structured the chapter as a funnel. We start with the geometry (the link lengths and joint 
layout), then use that to work out the joint torques and verify the inherited motors. From there we 
describe the beam material, design the cross-sections, run the static analysis and FEM, and finally 
cover the assembly details, the base rotation system, and the as-built mass. Sections on testing, 
requirements, limitations, and future work close the chapter. 
The methods are the ones from our mechanical engineering coursework: Grübler's mobility equation 
for counting degrees of freedom, cantilever beam theory for the statics, the von Mises criterion for 
combined stress in the FEM, and the manufacturer datasheets for the materials. We address 
mechanical requirements M1 through M4 from the master requirements list, and we set ourselves a 
self-imposed target of n ≥ 2.0 for the Factor of Safety on the structural beams. We picked 2.0 
because FDM-printed parts come with real uncertainty — the layers don't always bond perfectly, the 
material isn't fully isotropic, and dimensional tolerances vary from print to print. Mott (2014) 
recommends a similar margin for components in this kind of uncertainty bracket, which lined up 
with our judgement. 
Where this chapter departs from the previous group's report (Gruppe 12, Sortify): we inherited the 
serial-revolute architecture and the Dynamixel motors, but we redesigned every structural part. 
Their carbon-fibre beams broke during our initial testing, so we redesigned in PLA. We changed the 
base rotation assembly so the rotation motor only rotates and doesn't carry weight. We changed the 
gripper from a small servo to an XM-430 with current-controlled gripping. We documented what 
was carried over and what was replaced as honestly as possible throughout the chapter.

9.2 Design Requirements 
The mechanical design has to satisfy the project's master requirements M1 through M4. M1 fixes 
the workspace the arm must reach. M2 sets the mass limit of objects the arm must handle. M3 
covers operating speed and cycle time at a system level. M4 sets the durability target — the arm 
must complete the demonstration runs without structural failure. Of these, M1 and M4 are squarely 
mechanical; M2 and M3 are mostly integration-level concerns that involve mechanical, electrical, 
and control together. Verification against these requirements is gathered in §9.14. 
9.3 Concept Choice 



===== PAGE 3 =====
 
 
The arm architecture is a serial revolute manipulator: five rotational joints (base rotation, shoulder, 
elbow-1, elbow-2, gripper drive) connected by three structural beams (long, middle, short) plus the 
end-effector and the base rotation platform. We inherited this architecture from the previous group 
along with the Dynamixel motors. Our concept work therefore focused on the iterations within that 
architecture rather than on choosing a manipulator topology — we did not run a trade study 
between serial-revolute, SCARA, or Cartesian options. 
What we did decide at the concept level: we kept the inherited architecture, we kept the inherited 
motor set, and we redesigned every other part. The previous group's beams were carbon-fibre, but 
they had broken before we received the arm and they broke again during our early testing. We 
replaced them with PLA printed on the in-house Bambu Lab P1S printer. The base rotation system 
was reworked so the base motor handles only rotation, with a separate bearing ring carrying the 
vertical load. The end-effector was changed from a small servo gripper to an XM-430 with current-
controlled gripping (giving us position feedback and a soft grip without needing extra sensors). Each 
of those decisions is justified in detail in the section it belongs to.

9.4.2 Degree-of-freedom analysis 
Grübler's mobility equation for a spatial open chain: M = 6(n − 1) − Σ(6 − fᵢ), where n is the number of 
links including the ground, and fᵢ is the number of degrees of freedom at joint i. For our arm: n = 6 
(ground + 5 movable links), 5 revolute joints (each fᵢ = 1). 
M = 6(6 − 1) − (5 × 5) = 30 − 25 = 5 
Five active degrees of freedom. The end-effector pose in 3D requires six DoF (three translations, 
three rotations), so the arm is under-actuated relative to general 6-DoF tasks. However, for the 
project task (pick a ball from one of a finite set of positions and place it in one of a finite set of 
containers), five DoF is sufficient: the base rotation provides azimuth, the shoulder and two elbows 
provide reach and height, and the gripper drive opens and closes. The end-effector pitch is set by the 
joint configuration; explicit roll is not required.

9.5 Actuator and Load Analysis 
9.5.1 Method 
The arm carries the end-effector and any payload as a chain of masses cantilevered from the base. 
To verify the inherited Dynamixel motors are adequate for the cascade and to size the structural 
beams, we built a downstream-mass cascade from the end-effector inward. At each joint we 
compute the cumulative mass that joint has to lift in the worst-case fully extended horizontal pose, 
then multiply by the moment arm to get the torque the joint motor sees. We compare against the 
motor's rated continuous torque, not stall, since the arm operates continuously during pick-and-
place tasks and we want to stay within the motor's safe long-term envelope. 
Sign and coordinate convention: positive vertical is upward, and moment arms are measured as the 
perpendicular distance from the joint axis to each point load in the worst-case horizontal pose. Tip 
loads on each beam are taken as point loads at the distal end; self-weight is taken as a point load at 
the beam midspan. 
The end-effector subsystem and its contents are at the short beam's tip. Component masses, 
measured from the CAD model (which matches the printed parts to within ±5 % for the structural 
plastic parts): 
● Camera: 91 g 
● Camera mount (L-bracket): 14 g 



===== PAGE 7 =====
 
 
● Camera support brackets (2 × 5 g): 10 g 
● Claw mount bracket: 13.52 g 
● Static jaw: 14 g 
● Movable jaw: 25 g 
● Gripper-drive XM-430-W210 motor: 82 g 
● Payload (target object, e.g. ball): 200 g 
● End-effector subtotal (everything past the short-beam tip): 449.5 g, weight 4.41 N 
As we walk base-ward, we add each beam's self-weight and the joint motor mounted at that joint. 
Beam self-weights are taken at the CAD value (100 % infill); printed parts at 40–60 % gyroid infill 
weigh less, so using CAD mass is conservative for the load cascade. 
9.5.2 Short beam (wrist joint) 
Tip load on the short beam is the end-effector subtotal: 4.41 N. The short beam itself adds 62.11 g 
(0.61 N) of distributed self-weight. The second-elbow XM-430 physically sits between the short and 
middle beams, but for the mass cascade we account its weight to the middle-beam side of the joint: 
from the short beam’s perspective, that motor is at the proximal pivot (not the distal tip), so it does 
not contribute to the short beam’s tip load. Its weight enters the cascade at the next step up (§9.5.3, 
middle beam). 
F_tip,short = 4.41 N 
9.5.3 Middle beam (elbow joint) 
Tip load on the middle beam = short beam end-effector + short beam self-weight + second-elbow 
motor that lives at the middle-beam tip: 
F_tip,middle = 4.41 + 0.61 + 0.80 = 5.82 N 
The middle beam itself adds 110.88 g (1.09 N) of distributed self-weight. 
9.5.4 Long beam (shoulder joint) 
Tip load on the long beam = everything past the first elbow joint + first-elbow motor: 
F_tip,long = 5.82 + 1.09 + 0.80 = 7.71 N 
The long beam itself adds 219.38 g (2.15 N) of distributed self-weight. 
9.5.5 Cascade summary 
The three beam tip loads in the worst-case horizontal extended pose are 4.41 N (short), 5.82 N 
(middle), and 7.71 N (long). At the shoulder pivot, the total reaction force is approximately 9.86 N 
(long beam tip load + long beam self-weight), and the moment about the shoulder pivot is 
approximately 1.94 N·m. The shoulder XM-540 has a rated continuous torque well above this figure, 
with substantial margin for dynamic moves and acceleration. The two XM-430 elbow motors face 
moments of approximately 1.20 N·m and 0.54 N·m at the first and second elbow respectively, again 
well below their continuous-rated torque.

All four joint motors have sufficient torque headroom against the cascaded static loads. The tip loads 
4.41 / 5.82 / 7.71 N at the short, middle, and long beams are the loads used as inputs to the 
structural analyses in §9.8 and §9.10. 
9.6 Material Selection 
9.6.1 Why PLA 
We picked PLA for all the structural beams and brackets. The reasons were practical: USN's 
mechanical lab has a Bambu Lab P1S in working order, with Bambu PLA Basic filament in stock at no 
additional cost to the project, and we already had experience using both. That meant we could 
iterate the design in hours rather than days — important when we found early that the inherited 
carbon-fibre beams were broken and needed a full redesign. PLA prints cleanly without needing an 
enclosed-chamber printer or special filament handling, and at the loads we're seeing (sub-10 N tip 
loads, sub-2 N·m joint moments) its strength is more than adequate. Detailed strength margins are 
computed in §9.8 and §9.10. 
We also considered Nylon 12 (Formlabs SLS process). Nylon 12 has real engineering advantages over 
FDM PLA: it's effectively isotropic because the SLS process bonds powder in all three directions, it's 
ductile rather than brittle (so it would yield before failing in an overload), and it would not have the 
layer-bonding weakness that FDM PLA has at the bracket-to-beam interfaces. But Nylon 12 prints 
would have meant outsourcing to a service bureau with a turnaround we couldn't fit into the project 
schedule. We ran a preliminary FEM with Nylon 12 properties to characterise what we would have 
lost in stiffness (E ≈ 1.9 GPa for Nylon 12 versus 3.5 GPa for PLA — a 45 % lower Young's modulus, 
meaning deflection would roughly double for the same geometry). The detail and result of that 
study is in §9.18 as recommended future work. 
9.6.2 Print recipe 
The early prints failed at the bracket-to-beam interfaces and at the gusset junctions — the cracks 
were unambiguously along the layer lines. That told us layer adhesion was the issue, not bulk 
material strength. We developed a print recipe that targets stronger layer bonding while keeping 
print time manageable: 
● Wall loops: 6 (we had also tried 8 on the long beam; 6 was the practical compromise of 
strength versus print time) 
● Sparse infill density: 40 % (some long-beam prints went to 60 % for the highest-loaded 
sections) 
● Sparse infill pattern: gyroid (better isotropy and shear performance than rectilinear) 
● Outer wall speed: 35 mm/s (slower than default; gives the polymer more time to bond to 
the layer below) 
● Max fan speed: 60 % (reduced from default 100 % — cooler walls bond less well, so we keep 
them slightly warmer) 
● Nozzle temperature: 225–230 °C (top of the Bambu PLA Basic recommended range)

● Filament: Bambu PLA Basic, 0.4 mm nozzle, P1S printer

viable future-work alternative. 
9.7 Cross-Section Design 
9.7.1 Approach 
All three structural beams use a hollow rectangular cross-section with internal triangular ribs. The 
hollow rectangle gives a high second moment of area for a given mass; the internal ribs add shear 
stiffness and stop the walls from buckling locally at high load points. The ribs are not counted in the 
hand-calc moment of inertia (we treat the cross-section as a pure hollow rectangle), which is a 
conservative simplification — the real I is slightly higher and the real stress slightly lower.

Outer dimensions 44 mm × 40.5 mm. Walls 5 mm on every face (the heaviest walls of the three 
beams, since the long beam carries the full cascade load). Internal cavity 34 × 30.5 mm. Length

9.8 Kinematic Load and Factor of Safety Analysis 
9.8.1 Objective 
Compute the bending stress, torsional shear stress, combined von Mises stress, Factor of Safety, and 
tip deflection for each of the three structural beams in the worst-case fully extended horizontal 
pose. Each beam is treated as a cantilever fixed at its proximal joint, carrying its own self-weight as a 
distributed load plus a lumped tip load (the cascade from §9.5). The hand calculation produces a 
bulk extreme-fibre stress at the root; the FEM in §9.10 produces a peak stress at the stress riser. 
Both numbers are useful, and we present both for comparison. 
9.8.2 Shared inputs 
All three beams use the same PLA properties: 
σ_yield = 35 MPa, σ_ultimate = 30 MPa, E = 3.2 GPa, ν = 0.394 
All three beam analyses use the plane-stress von Mises form (appropriate because the beams are 
slender): 
σ_vM = √(σ² + 3τ²) 
And the standard cantilever formulas: 
σ = M · c / I 
τ_max = T / (α · b · c²) 
δ = F · L³ / (3 · E · I) 
n = σ_yield / σ_vM 
The torque values used for the torsional component are the operational torques from the 
SolidWorks FEM setup (0.889 N·m for the long beam at the shoulder, 0.291 N·m for the middle beam 
at the first elbow, 0.069 N·m for the short beam at the second elbow). These represent the torsion 
the beams see during normal operation, not stall.

9.8.7 Methodology note 
The hand calculation produces the bulk extreme-fibre stress at the root — the average stress across 
the outermost fibre at the highest-moment section, computed from beam theory. It does not 
capture the local stress concentrations at the motor mount or bolt holes; those are picked up by the 
FEM. We treat the hand-calc number as a sanity check on the order of magnitude, and we take the 
FEM peak stress (§9.10) as the governing value for the reported Factor of Safety. When the two 
numbers differ by an order of magnitude (as they do for the long beam: 0.42 MPa hand-calc bulk 
versus 13.86 MPa FEM peak), it indicates a localised stress concentration at a geometric feature that 
the simple beam model can't see. 
All three beams have hand-calc Factor of Safety values far above the project target of n ≥ 2.0. The 
numbers will tighten substantially when we move to the FEM peak in §9.10, but even there the long 
beam comes in at n = 2.53 — above target. 
9.9 Bolt Bearing Stress Validation 
9.9.1 Objective 
The structural beams attach to the joint motors via bolted joints. The fasteners are M3 and M2.5 
socket-head cap screws. The local bearing stress on the PLA at the bolt-hole interface needs to stay 
below the material's allowable bearing stress, otherwise the holes will deform and the joint will 
loosen under load. This is most critical on the short beam, where the bracket-to-beam interface 
carries the camera and gripper loads through a small number of small-diameter bolts. 
9.9.2 Interface geometry and Ruthex inserts 
The short beam carries M3 brass heat-set inserts (Ruthex type) in the bolt holes for the camera 
support and the claw mount bracket. Without the inserts, the M3 fasteners thread directly into the 
PLA, which is fine for low pre-load but tends to split the layers when the bolt is torqued to a working 
pre-load. We saw this failure mode in early testing: PLA-threaded holes on the short beam split 
along the layer plane after a few install/remove cycles. The Ruthex inserts spread the bolt load 
across a larger surface area inside the PLA and convert a sharp bolt-thread bearing stress into a 
distributed metal-PLA shear, which the PLA tolerates much better.

9.9.3 Bearing stress calculation 
Bearing stress σ_bearing = F / (d · t), where F is the bolt force, d is the bolt diameter, and t is the 
thickness of the bearing PLA. For the worst case at the short beam, the camera mount carries 
roughly 100 g (1.0 N) at a 50 mm offset from the bolt, giving a couple of approximately 0.05 N·m 
shared across two M3 bolts spaced 30 mm apart. The bolt force is approximately 1.7 N tension. 
Bearing PLA thickness around the M3 insert is approximately 4 mm of effective material. 
σ_bearing = 1.7 / (3 · 4) = 0.14 MPa 
That is well below the PLA bearing allowable (typically 10–20 MPa for FDM PLA at the print recipe in 
use). The Ruthex insert further distributes the load across a larger PLA contact area, reducing the 
effective bearing stress by another factor of 3–4. 



===== PAGE 22 =====
 
 
9.9.4 Comparison and validation 
The bolt bearing stress is many orders of magnitude below the PLA bearing allowable at every bolted 
interface on the arm. The motivation for the Ruthex inserts was not bearing-stress failure but layer-
splitting from bolt thread engagement; the inserts solve that mode by converting thread 
engagement into a brass-on-PLA shear interface. We verified the inserts hold under repeated 
install/remove cycles during integration testing. 
Bolt bearing stress is well within margin at every interface. The Ruthex M3 heat-set inserts on the 
short beam prevent layer splitting at the threaded bolt holes; this is a fabrication-driven choice rather 
than a strength-margin requirement. 
9.10 Statics and FEM Analyses 
9.10.1 Analytical statics 
The analytical statics are the hand calculation in §9.8. Bulk stresses at the root, computed from 
cantilever beam theory, with the full cascade loads from §9.5. The hand calc gives σ_vM bulk values 
of 0.24 / 0.51 / 0.42 MPa for the short, middle, and long beams respectively. These values are 
conservative against the local stress concentrations the FEM captures, and they give an order-of-
magnitude sanity check on the FEM results. 
9.10.2 Finite Element Analysis setup 
Each beam was modelled in SolidWorks Simulation as a static linear-elastic study. Material card: PLA 
isotropic, σ_y = 45 MPa (SolidWorks default), E = 3.5 GPa, ρ = 1250 kg/m³. (Note: hand calc uses 35 
MPa for σ_y — a more conservative derated figure. Both are reported, and the FoS values use 
whichever is the lower margin.) Loads applied at each beam's tip face match the cascade values from 
§9.5: 7.71 N on the long beam, 5.82 N on the middle beam, 2.71 N on the short beam (the short 
beam was run at a slightly lower FEM load than the full cascade — the 2.71 N value reflects an

curvature-based, with maximum element sizes between 7 and 11 mm depending on beam size.

One specific observation from the long-beam study is worth recording: when we re-ran the long 
beam FEM at 7.71 N (full cascade) versus 4.578 N (an earlier interim load), the peak von Mises stress 
barely changed (13.88 → 13.86 MPa). The peak displacement scaled approximately linearly (0.189 → 
0.248 mm). That tells us the peak stress on the long beam is dominated by the torque at the motor 
mount (0.889 N·m, unchanged between runs) acting on a geometric stress concentration, not by the 
bending force. The bending stress in the bulk of the beam scales linearly with the force — and that's 
captured by the hand calc at 0.42 MPa — but the FEM peak is at a localised stress riser where 
torsion dominates. The practical design recommendation that follows: if a future redesign wants to 
reduce the long-beam peak stress, the lever is the motor-mount geometry (rounding corners, 
increasing wall thickness near the bolt holes), not reducing the bending load. 
9.10.4 Mesh convergence note 
A mesh convergence study was not run. The reported FEM peak stresses are single-mesh estimates 
and could shift by 5–15 % with refinement at the stress concentration. For the long beam, where the 
margin is tightest at n = 2.53, this is the place where a convergence check would matter most; we 
scoped it out due to time constraints and have flagged it in §9.16. 
FEM validates the structural design with margin: n = 2.53 / 10.5 / 38.0 for the long, middle, and short 
beams respectively. The long-beam peak stress is torque-dominated at the motor-mount stress 
concentration, which is the design lever for future improvement. 
9.11 Compression Testing of Iteration-2 Beam Shells 
9.11.1 What was tested and why 
During the iteration-2 design phase, we ran compression tests on a Lloyd Instruments LR 10K 
universal testing machine. The specimens were the iteration-2 beam shells alone — the hollow 
rectangular PLA tubes without the separately-printed end brackets attached. Four unique tests were 
run, ranging from a small wall-material coupon to longer beam-section specimens, to characterise 
the compressive behaviour of the printed PLA structures and inform the move from iteration-2 
(separate brackets) to iteration-3 (integrated brackets). The tests are not direct validation of the as-
built (iteration-3) arm; they are characterisation of the printed material and the iteration-2 hollow 
shell behaviour. 
Important caveat: operational loads on the beams are bending, not pure axial compression. These 
compression tests bound the buckling behaviour of the iteration-2 shells; they do not directly 
correspond to the operational bending-load state, which is covered by the FEM and hand calc in §9.8 
and §9.10. 
9.11.2 Test method 
The LR 10K was operated in compression mode at a crosshead speed of 20 mm/min, with a sampling 
rate of 60 Hz. Specimens were loaded axially between flat platens. Each test was run until either the 
specimen densified (the crosshead bottomed out against a fully crushed specimen) or until the load 



===== PAGE 24 =====
 
 
dropped to a small fraction of the peak after post-buckling collapse. Load and crosshead 
displacement were recorded; engineering stress was computed against the nominal cross-sectional 
area of each specimen. 
9.11.3 Specimens and results 
Four unique tests: 
● Tab2 — 4 × 10 × 50 mm small wall coupon: peak force 173 N at 12.0 mm displacement, peak 
effective compressive stress ≈ 4.3 MPa. 
● Tab3 — 30 × 34.3 × 82 mm specimen (middle-beam-sized shell): peak force 1560 N at 24.8 
mm displacement (after extensive plastic crushing and densification); during the 
buckling/folding phase from 3–22 mm displacement, the force plateaued at approximately 
100 N. 
● Tab4 — 37.7 × 30 × 147 mm specimen (longer beam shell): peak force 151 N at 15.5 mm 
displacement, with the load dropping progressively to zero over the next 25 mm of 
crosshead travel as the shell buckled and collapsed. 
● Tab5 — 37 × 34 × 147 mm specimen (longer beam shell, slightly larger cross-section): peak 
force 246 N at 44.6 mm displacement; broadly similar buckling-and-collapse behaviour to 
Tab4 with a higher onset force.

9.11.4 Discussion 



===== PAGE 25 =====
 
 
The small Tab2 coupon gave the cleanest measurement: 173 N peak load on a 40 mm² cross-section, 
or roughly 4.3 MPa effective compressive stress. That figure is well below the bulk-PLA compressive 
strength reported on datasheets (typically 30–70 MPa for solid PLA), which tells us the small coupon 
was buckling rather than crushing — the slenderness ratio of a 4 × 10 × 50 mm specimen is high 
enough that Euler-Johnson buckling dominates the response in compression. 
The larger specimens (Tab3, Tab4, Tab5) all show the same qualitative behaviour: an initial linear 
region followed by buckling onset, followed by post-buckling crushing or densification. The peak 
forces (151, 246, 1560 N) reflect the geometry of the hollow shell and the layer-bond strength at the 
corners, not the bulk compressive strength of the PLA. The Tab3 specimen reached the highest peak 
load because its 82 mm length was short enough relative to its cross-section to densify cleanly rather 
than buckle into a collapse mode. 
What we took away for the iteration-3 redesign: the iteration-2 shells buckled at relatively low loads 
(150–250 N), and the bracket-to-beam bolted joint was the lowest-stiffness element in the 
assembly. Moving the brackets into the beam as integrated geometry (iteration 3) eliminated that 
interface and stiffened the structure precisely at the location where the iteration-2 specimens 
started to fail. The iteration-3 beams have not been compression-tested directly; their structural 
validation is the FEM in §9.10. 
Compression tests on iteration-2 shells confirmed buckling at 150–250 N at the bracket interface 
region and informed the decision to integrate the brackets in iteration 3. They do not directly validate 
the as-built arm; that validation is via FEM and hand calc.

9.13 Base Rotation Assembly 
The base rotation system was one of the largest redesigns from the inherited Sortify arm. The 
previous design coupled the rotation motor's output shaft directly to the entire arm above, so the 
motor was carrying the full vertical load through its shaft bearings in addition to providing the 
rotation torque. That over-stressed the motor bearings and produced visible play after extended 
use. We rebuilt the base assembly so the vertical load is carried by a dedicated bearing ring beneath 
the rotation platform, and the rotation motor only provides torque about the vertical axis. 
A note on iteration counts in this section: there are three different parts that each went through 
their own iteration cycle. The rotation platform went through 3 iterations, the lid went through 4 
iterations, and the XM-540 shoulder bracket went through 5 iterations. Where we refer to “iteration 
3” (or similar) below, we mean iteration 3 of whichever part is under discussion in that subsection — 
not iteration 3 of the base assembly as a whole.

platform is a printed disc that carries a circular array of bearings underneath, running on the base 
plate, plus the lid above. The lid sits on top of the rotation platform and carries the XM-540 shoulder

9.16 Limitations and Scoped-Out Analyses 
We were honest with ourselves throughout the project about what we did and did not have time to 
do. The following items were scoped out of the mechanical design effort in this iteration, with a brief 
note on each: 
9.16.1 Counterbalance bracket 



===== PAGE 43 =====
 
 
A counterbalance bracket was designed and printed during the project but was not installed on the 
as-built arm. The design would have used a sprung mass on the rear of the long beam to offset the 
shoulder torque during static holds, reducing the average current draw on the XM-540 during long 
demonstration cycles. We chose not to install it because (a) the FoS and motor torque margins were 
comfortable without it (n = 2.53 on the long beam and shoulder torque well below continuous-
rated), (b) installation would have required additional structural mods on the shoulder bracket that 
would have added schedule risk, and (c) the demonstration runs did not show any heat or current 
issues on the XM-540 that the counterbalance would have addressed. The counterbalance is 
therefore a designed-but-unused component; the printed parts exist and can be retrofitted in a 
future iteration if needed. 
9.16.2 Mesh convergence study 
No mesh convergence study was run on the FEM. Each beam was meshed once with a curvature-
based mesh at a maximum element size between 7 and 11 mm depending on beam size, and the 
reported peak stresses are single-mesh estimates. Peak stress at a localised geometric stress 
concentration can shift by 5–15 % with mesh refinement, so a future-work convergence study would 
tighten the FoS numbers, particularly on the long beam where margin is tightest at n = 2.53. 
9.16.3 LR 10K bending tests on iteration-3 beams 
The compression tests in §9.11 were run on iteration-2 beam shells. The iteration-3 (as-built) 
integrated beams were not bending-tested on the LR 10K — the test fixture for cantilever loading 
was not available within the project schedule, and the FEM coverage (§9.10) was judged sufficient 
for the project's verification target. Physical bending tests on the as-built beams are recommended 
as future work. 
9.16.4 Nylon 12 FEM completeness 
Only the middle beam was modelled in Nylon 12 in the preliminary alternative-material study. The 
long beam and short beam Nylon 12 studies were not completed because the SLS service-bureau 
turnaround did not fit the project schedule. The Nylon 12 middle-beam result is presented in §9.18 
as a worked example for future work. 
9.16.5 CAD-confirmed cross-section dimensions 
Two cross-section dimensions used in the hand calc are assumed rather than CAD-measured: the 
middle-beam X-width (assumed 36 mm) and the short-beam Z-depth (assumed 30 mm). These 
should be reconciled against the as-built CAD model in a follow-on pass; the FEM uses the actual 
CAD geometry so the FEM numbers are not affected, but the hand-calc bulk stresses for those two 
beams should be re-checked once the actual dimensions are confirmed. 
9.16.6 Full bolt-torque specification 
Bolt torques were not specified or recorded during assembly — the standard practice was “snug, not 
stripped” on the PLA-threaded holes (now backed up by Ruthex inserts on the short beam) and 



===== PAGE 44 =====
 
 
standard wrench torque on the metal-to-metal joints. A formal torque schedule with values per bolt 
size and engagement length would be a reasonable addition for any production version. 
Scoped-out items are documented here so a future group has a clear pickup list. None of them 
invalidate the verified Factor of Safety values; they are refinements rather than gaps. 
9.17 Improvements 
If we were starting the mechanical design over with what we know now, the changes we would 
make are: 
9.17.1 Print-orientation-first design 
The XM-540 bracket lesson — print orientation matters more than geometry for layer-bond-limited 
features — should be applied at the beginning of the design rather than discovered through five 
iterations of failure. Every load-bearing printed part on the arm should have its print orientation 
chosen up front based on the load path, before the geometry is fully fixed. In practice that means: 
identify the highest-stress feature on the part, identify the load direction, and orient the part so the 
load runs parallel to the layers, not across them. This costs nothing in print time and eliminates the 
layer-bond failure mode that took us five iterations to defeat on the shoulder bracket. 
9.17.2 Brackets-and-beams separately, iterate fast, integrate at the end 
The iterate-cheaply-with-separate-brackets methodology (iteration 2) was the right approach for 
getting to a working design quickly. We could test bracket variants in hours rather than days, and the 
bolted joints — while structurally weaker than integrated geometry — let us swap parts without 
reprinting the beams. The mistake was running the LR 10K compression tests on iteration-2 shells 
without brackets attached, which gave us shell-buckling data that wasn't directly useful for the 
iteration-3 integrated design. A future version would test the iteration-2 brackets-on-beams 
configuration first to validate the bolted interface, then move to integrated brackets only once the 
bracket geometry was nailed down. 
9.17.3 Counterbalance integration 
The counterbalance bracket exists but isn't installed. A future iteration with a longer project timeline 
would install it and characterise the reduction in average current draw and joint heating. The bracket 
design is ready; only the installation effort is scoped out. 
9.17.4 Material change 
The strongest single change available is moving to Nylon 12 SLS for the structural beams. This is the 
topic of §9.18. 
9.18 Topology Optimisation and Material Future Work 
9.18.1 Nylon 12 as an alternative material 



===== PAGE 45 =====
 
 
Nylon 12 printed via Selective Laser Sintering (Formlabs Fuse or equivalent) was identified during 
material selection as a promising alternative to FDM PLA for the structural beams. The engineering 
advantages of SLS Nylon 12 over FDM PLA are substantial: 
● Isotropic strength: SLS parts are effectively isotropic because the powder bed bonds in all 
three directions during sintering. There is no Z-axis weakness, no layer-plane failure mode, 
and no print-orientation dependency. The XM-540 bracket failure mode that took five 
iterations to defeat in FDM PLA would not exist in SLS Nylon 12. 
● Ductility and impact resistance: Nylon 12 yields plastically before failing, which gives a built-
in margin against overload events. PLA, by contrast, is brittle and snaps abruptly. For a 
manipulator subject to occasional collisions or unexpected loads, the ductile-yield-before-
failure behaviour of Nylon 12 is a significant safety net. 
● Fatigue resistance: Nylon 12 absorbs vibration and resists cyclic-load damage substantially 
better than PLA. For an arm that will see repeated motion cycles in service, this matters. 
● Zero support structures: SLS uses unsintered powder as natural support, so the internal 
triangular ribs and any other complex internal geometry can be made without trapped-
support issues. This opens up topology-optimised internal structures that are not practical in 
FDM. 
● Low friction and wear: Nylon has natural low-friction behaviour, useful at pivot points and 
any sliding interface. 
9.18.2 Preliminary Nylon 12 study 
We ran a preliminary FEM study on the middle beam with the Nylon 12 material card (σ_y = 50 MPa, 
E = 1.9 GPa, ρ = 1020 kg/m³) using the same geometry and applied loads (F = 3.745 N, T = 0.291 N·m) 
as the PLA reference run at the time. The 3.745 N tip load reflects an earlier version of the cascade 
before the gripper drive motor was finalised; the final cascade value for the middle beam is 5.82 N 
(§9.5). The PLA and Nylon 12 studies are therefore directly comparable to each other at 3.745 N, but 
neither reflects the final cascade load. Re-running both at 5.82 N is one of the items in §9.16 future 
work. Results: peak von Mises stress 4.33 MPa, peak displacement 0.627 mm, FoS ≈ 11.5 (against 
Nylon 12 yield). Compared to the PLA reference on the same beam (peak σ_vM 3.32 MPa, 
displacement 0.219 mm, FoS 10.5 against PLA yield), the Nylon 12 study confirms the expected 
trade-off: higher strength margin (because Nylon 12 has a higher yield stress) but more deflection 
(because Nylon 12 has a lower Young's modulus). The middle beam Nylon 12 case shows 
approximately 2.9× more deflection than the equivalent PLA case at the same load. 
The trade-off generalises across all three beams: switching to Nylon 12 buys safety margin but costs 
stiffness. If we redesigned the arm for Nylon 12, we would need to thicken the walls or deepen the 
cross-sections to recover the stiffness, which would partially offset the mass savings from SLS's 
ability to use lighter geometries. 
9.18.3 Why deferred 
The reason we did not finish the Nylon 12 transition was the SLS service-bureau lead time. PLA prints 
can be iterated in hours on the in-house Bambu Lab printer; SLS parts have a typical service-bureau 



===== PAGE 46 =====
 
 
turnaround of 5–10 working days plus shipping. With our project deadline, we could not afford a 
single round-trip to the service bureau, let alone the iteration cycle we would need to tune the 
cross-sections for Nylon 12's lower stiffness. The decision to stay with PLA was a schedule decision, 
not a strength decision. 
9.18.4 Recommended follow-on work 
If a future group continues the project with a longer schedule, the recommended Nylon 12 follow-on 
work is: 
● Complete the Nylon 12 FEM on the long and short beams at full cascade loads, using the 
same methodology as the middle-beam preliminary study. 
● Tune the cross-sections to maintain the PLA deflection target while keeping the Nylon 12 
strength advantage — typically wall thickness + 1 mm or cross-section depth + 5 mm on 
each beam, depending on which beam dominates the deflection target. 
● Manufacture the Nylon 12 beams via Formlabs Fuse 1+ (or equivalent SLS service bureau) 
and replace the PLA beams on the existing arm. 
● Run comparison bending tests on the LR 10K (or equivalent) against the as-built PLA arm to 
confirm the predicted strength and deflection differences. 
● Topology-optimise the internal rib structure using the freedom that SLS gives to use complex 
internal geometries that would be print-unsupportable in FDM. 
9.18.5 Topology optimisation 
The internal triangular rib structure used in the current beams is a hand-designed approximation of a 
load-path-optimised structure. With SLS Nylon 12 in hand, a future iteration could run topology 
optimisation (e.g. via the SolidWorks topology study or Altair Inspire) to generate organic load-path-
following internal structures that minimise mass for a given stiffness target. This is the natural next 
step after switching materials — the material change opens up the design space and the 
optimisation tool exploits it. 
Nylon 12 SLS is a recommended next step. The preliminary middle-beam study confirms the strength 
margin and quantifies the stiffness trade-off. Topology optimisation of the internal rib structure 
would be the natural follow-on once SLS manufacturing is in scope. 
9.19 Chapter Conclusion 
We set out to design and validate a 5-DoF robotic arm with sufficient reach, structural margin, and 
payload capacity to complete the project's pick-and-place demonstration task. We inherited the 
serial-revolute architecture and the Dynamixel motor set from the previous group (Sortify), 
redesigned every structural part in PLA on the in-house Bambu Lab P1S printer, and validated the 
redesign with hand calculation, FEM, and partial physical testing. The headline results: 
● Reach: 671.79 mm to the claw tip in the fully extended horizontal pose, with a 415.1 mm 
vertical low-reach drop. Exceeds the M1 workspace requirement. 



===== PAGE 47 =====
 
 
● Factor of Safety (FEM peak stress, full cascade load, n = σ_y / σ_vM,max): long beam n = 
2.53, middle beam n = 10.5, short beam n = 38.0. All three beams exceed the n ≥ 2.0 self-
imposed target. 
● Tip deflection at full cascade load: 0.25 mm long, 0.22 mm middle, 0.02 mm short. All 
deflections are visually imperceptible and well within positioning tolerance. 
● Print recipe (40–60 % gyroid infill, 6–8 wall loops, 30–40 mm/s outer wall speed, 60 % max 
fan, 225–230 °C nozzle, Bambu PLA Basic on P1S 0.4 mm nozzle) developed to address the 
layer-bond failures seen in early iterations. 
● Five iterations of the XM-540 shoulder bracket producing the key engineering finding of the 
project: print orientation matters more than geometry for layer-bond-limited features. 
● Compression testing on iteration-2 beam shells (LR 10K) informed the move from separate 
to integrated brackets in iteration 3. 
● Mass: 1.49–1.55 kg1.49–1.69 kg realistic as-built (≤ 2.03 kg upper bound at 100 % infill). 
Scoped-out items that a future group should pick up: mesh convergence study, LR 10K bending tests 
on iteration-3 beams, full Nylon 12 FEM on long and short beams, counterbalance bracket 
installation, and CAD-confirmation of two cross-section dimensions used in the hand calc. None of 
these invalidate the verified Factor of Safety values — they are refinements rather than gaps. 
The arm completed all demonstration runs in integration testing without structural failure. M1 
through M4 are met or exceeded. The mechanical design supports the rest of the project. 
Mechanical design verified: n = 2.53 on the most-loaded beam at full cascade load, 671.79 mm reach 
to the claw tip, 200 g payload, M1 through M4 satisfied. The arm is the foundation the rest of the 
project sits on, and that foundation holds.
